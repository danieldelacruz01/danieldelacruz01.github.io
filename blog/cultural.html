Emotional Intelligence

In preparation for this cultural reflection, I read the first chapter of Google's Search Inside Yourself: The Unexpected Path to Achieving Success, Happiness (and World Peace) by Chade-Meng Tan, and had a go at practicing mindfulness for two minutes.

What learnings did you take from the chapter?

I was a little bit familiar with the book already, after seeing a short lecture on emotional intelligence at EDA's 'Day in the Life' open day. I think what I found most interesting was the link between our psychological and physiological well-being. Our emotions are correlated with different parts of the body, and changes in our emotions result in bodily changes too. Therefore, by enhancing our perceptibility to emotional changes, we are able to better control our physiological well-being. It seems obvious reading about it now, but it's something I hadn't actively thought about before.

How did you find the two minutes of mindfulness?

I found it quite difficult to make such a sudden shift from 'doing' to 'being'. It's something that becomes easier the more you practice it, I suppose. Shifting your focus back onto your breathing helps when you find your mind wandering. When the two minutes were up, I came out feeling like I was 'gone' for longer than two minutes and felt a little bit disorientated! But it felt good to clear my head a bit and I'll definitely try practicing mindfulness on a more regular basis.


05/12/2015

Shereef's Fireside Chat Reflection

What's your take on the DBC / EDA experience?

I view EDA as a course that challenges more traditional teaching and learning philosophies where, instead of simply being told what to learn and do, students are encouraged to take control of what/how they learn the material.

What are your impressions?

My initial impressions are really positive! So far, my learning has been really practical and 'hands-on'. I am able learn the material at a speed at which I am comfortable, using the helpful resources provided by EDA as well as other resources I have found independently.

How do you see yourself engaging with this type of culture?

Really well, I hope! Part of what attracted me to EDA was their focus on teaching soft skills, as well as allowing students the freedom to learn the material in a way that best suits their learning style. I see a lot of similarities between the way EDA is structured and the Montessori teaching pedagogy (which I am very familiar with as a former Montessori student). I see myself engaging the EDA culture really well, and look forward to what the next 18 weeks will bring!

Have your expectations of EDA changed? If so, how?

My expectations have definitely changed a little bit now that we have actually started. I was initially concerned that I might struggle to keep up with the material and was worried that it might be too technical for me to grasp. But the so far the way the course has been structured (with a number of assignments and smaller sub-tasks within the assignments) has meant that even if I might not complete a task in one sitting, I can still walk away feeling like I've accomplished something. It's also comforting to know that everyone else in my cohort is in the same boat as me, so if I'm struggling with something, chances are that someone else is struggling with it too.

Are you excited to participate in this kind of learning environment? Does it make you nervous?

As I mentioned before, I was intially pretty nervous about what to expect, but now that we have actually gotten stuck into it a little bit my nerves have setttled a bit!

Time and Habits

After reading through the resources on time management and developing good habits, I found the blog post on counterintuitive ways to be productive really interesting because of how much it challenged my understanding of what it means to be 'productive'. I always considered productivity to mean being able to accomplish a lot of tasks in any given day, but the post describes it more as a state of mental well-being. In other words, the work we do is only productive if it is meaningful to us and makes us feel good at the end of the day. I also like that the author writes about how the things that we do aren't always productive, but that doesn't mean they're not important

Although in the past I haven't really consciously thought about what techniques I use to be productive, I can see some parallels between what I just read about time & habits and what has worked for me in the past when working on a project. For example, starting small is a technique that I have used subconsciously when working on big projects like university assignments. I find that the task doesn't seem as dauting when you're only looking at small pieces at a time. However, it can be easy to put too much energy into one small part if you have a good flow going. I believe this is where timeboxing comes into play.

I see myself working on integrating timeboxing techniques into my work habits, as this will allow me to focus and, more importantly, de-focus on tasks in a more evenly distributed way. Toggl, the time-tracking tool used at EDA, has an in-built pomodoro timer feature which I will try out to see if it works for me. I also anticipate that myself and the teams I work with will be using the concept of Minimum Viable Product a lot throughout our EDA journey.
